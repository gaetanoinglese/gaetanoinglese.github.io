Welcome, thanks for visiting!

I am a PhD Candidate at the Government Department of the London School of Economics and Political Science, where I work under the supervision of Professor Sara B. Hobolt, and Professor Christopher J. Anderson. My PhD is funded by the Economic and Social Research Council (ESRC) .

My research examines the political implications of economic insecurity in Western Europe, with a specific focus on young and highly educated individuals.
My dissertation investigates three central challenges faced by young and highly educated individuals in today’s knowledge economies: declining income returns to education, the impact of Artificial Intelligence (AI), and housing unaffordability.
I argue that these pressures fuel discontent with the market economy and detachment from mainstream politics, especially during the formative years of political socialisation, creating lasting effects on political behaviour. Building on cross-disciplinary insights from political sociology, labour economics, and political behaviour, my research identifies the expectation-reality gap as a core mechanism behind political responses to economic insecurity.
Employing panel surveys and administrative data, I implement both observational and design-based methodologies with a strong focus on causal inference.

PhD Papers

From Aspiration to Disillusion? The Political Consequences of AI-driven Employment Threats
draft IBEI AI & Politics Workshop 2025 slides
Abstract
The expansion of higher education in knowledge economies has fostered the belief that investment in education brings economic rewards and enhances security. However, rapid advances in AI are transforming highly skilled sectors, increasing perceived risk among graduates. This paper tests whether AI-driven threats to employability erode meritocratic beliefs and shift economic preferences and vote intentions. Using survey data linked to UK job-vacancy admin data and new AI-exposure indices, and leveraging the December 2022 ChatGPT shock, I show that negative shifts reduce perceived fairness, move ideology leftward, and raise anti-incumbent intentions.
Aspirational, Still? Local House Price Increases and Political Preferences
Abstract
Housing prices are surging, intensifying affordability concerns—especially for the young. Using a shift-share instrument based on local stock composition and regional price trends, panel data, and IV estimates for the UK, I show that exogenous price increases strengthen anti-redistributive attitudes among homeowners, while renters exposed to rising prices show greater support for Reform UK, revealing a widening political cleavage between asset-owners and renters.
Unmet Expectations and Attitudes toward the Role of Government
Abstract
Using 30 waves of BHPS–Understanding Society, I show that early-life unmet income expectations predict support for economic intervention more strongly than realised earnings, especially among the highly educated and privileged backgrounds.

Summary
